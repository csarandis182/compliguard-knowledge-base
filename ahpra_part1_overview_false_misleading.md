# AHPRA Guidelines for Advertising a Regulated Health Service — Part 1: Overview, Scope & False/Misleading Claims

**Source:** Guidelines for Advertising a Regulated Health Service — AHPRA & National Boards  
**Date:** December 2020  
**Legislative basis:** Section 133 of the Health Practitioner Regulation National Law  
**chunk_id:** ahpra-part1-overview-false-misleading

---

## Who These Guidelines Apply To

These guidelines apply to **anyone advertising a regulated health service**, including:
- Registered health practitioners
- Individuals who are not registered health practitioners
- Businesses, partnerships and corporate entities

A **regulated health service** is a service provided by, or usually provided by, a registered health practitioner. Regulated health professions include: medicine, nursing, midwifery, pharmacy, physiotherapy, psychology, occupational therapy, optometry, dental, chiropractic, osteopathy, podiatry, paramedicine, medical radiation practice, Chinese medicine, and Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander health practice.

---

## What Is Advertising? (Section 3.1)

Advertising includes **all forms of verbal, printed or electronic public communication** that promotes a regulated health service provider to attract a person to the provider. This includes:
- Television, cinema, radio
- Newspapers, flyers, billboards
- Websites and social media (including Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn)
- Business cards, office signs, letterheads on public-facing documents
- Public and professional directory listings
- Books promoting a particular regulated health service provider
- Media reports, magazine articles or advertorials where the practitioner promotes their own service

**Not considered advertising:**
- Material issued to patients during consultations providing clinical/technical information (with opportunity to ask questions)
- Public health information or public health programs
- Patient comments on social media or review sites the practitioner does not control
- Promotion of a profession, training or education for a profession

---

## Who Is an Advertiser? (Section 3.2)

The person or entity who **controls** part or all of the advertising is the advertiser. An advertiser has control if:
- They publish or authorise content, or direct someone to publish or draft content (including third parties, staff, or marketing agencies); OR
- There is a mechanism for the advertiser to modify or remove content published by an unrelated publisher.

> **Compliance note:** Marketing agencies producing content for health services are acting on behalf of the advertiser. The registered health practitioner or business owner remains legally responsible for compliance.

---

## The Five Advertising Requirements (Section 133 of the National Law)

If you are advertising a regulated health service, your advertising must NOT:

1. Be **false, misleading or deceptive**, or likely to be misleading or deceptive
2. Offer a **gift, discount or other inducement** to attract a person to use the service, unless the terms and conditions of the offer are also stated
3. Use **testimonials or purported testimonials** about the service or business
4. Create an **unreasonable expectation of beneficial treatment**
5. Directly or indirectly **encourage the indiscriminate or unnecessary use** of regulated health services

**Penalties (Section 133 of the National Law):**
- Individual: up to **$5,000 per offence**
- Body corporate: up to **$10,000 per offence**
- Protected title misuse: up to **$60,000 per offence / 3 years imprisonment** (individual); up to **$120,000** (body corporate)

---

## Section 4.1 — False, Misleading or Deceptive Advertising

Advertising may be false, misleading or deceptive when it:
- Misleads, either directly or by implication through the use of emphasis, comparison, contrast or **omission**
- Provides **partial information** and/or omits important details
- Uses scientific information that is **inaccurate, unbalanced, not easily understood by the public**, or does not clearly identify researchers, sponsors and the academic publication
- Makes **statements about the effectiveness of the treatment that are not supported by acceptable evidence**
- Makes **unqualified claims about effectiveness** by listing health conditions that a treatment or service can "assist with" or "treat"
- Suggests a practitioner is a registered health practitioner or holds specialist registration **when they do not**
- **Minimises, underplays or under-represents the risk** or potential risk associated with a treatment or procedure
- Makes **comparisons without complete information**
- Makes claims about providing a **superior regulated health service**

---

## Section 4.1.1 — Evidence Required for Effectiveness Claims

Advertisers must be able to **substantiate claims** made in advertising. This is referred to as "acceptable evidence."

**Key distinction:** The evidence required for advertising claims is different from the evidence used for clinical decisions. Advertising is generic — patients cannot ask questions or receive individualised assessment. Therefore the evidentiary bar is higher.

### Section 4.1.2 — What Is Acceptable Evidence?

- Highest level: well-conducted **systematic review of relevant randomised controlled trials**
- Where a systematic review is unavailable: all relevant sources of evidence must be considered (research cannot be "cherry picked")

**Types of studies generally NOT considered acceptable evidence for advertising claims:**
- Studies involving no human subjects
- Before and after studies with few or no controls
- Self-assessment studies
- Anecdotal evidence based on observations in practice
- Outcome studies or audits (unless bias or other factors that may influence results are carefully controlled)
- Studies that are not applicable to the target population

---

## Section 4.1.3 — Comparative Advertising

Comparative advertising that promotes one regulated health service over another can be misleading because it is difficult to include complete information. If comparative claims are used, they must be:
- **Clear and accurate**
- **Supported by acceptable evidence** where relevant

Examples of comparative advertising:
- Comparisons between health outcomes and quality of care offered by different regulated health services
- Comparing professions, or the competency, skill or experience of practitioners
- Price comparisons of regulated health services

---

## Section 4.1.4 — Titles and Claims About Registration, Competence and Qualifications

### 4.1.4.a — Specialist Titles and Endorsements

Only a registered health practitioner who **holds specialist registration** in a recognised specialty or an **endorsement** may use the relevant specialist title in advertising. It is prohibited to:
- Claim to be a "specialist" or use a recognised specialty title when not holding specialist registration
- Claim to hold an endorsement when you do not hold the relevant endorsement

### 4.1.4.b — Other Uses of Specialist Terms

Use of words such as **"specialist", "specialises in", "specialty" or "specialised"** in advertising by a practitioner without specialist registration is likely to mislead the public.

Acceptable alternatives:
- "substantial experience in" ✓
- "working primarily in" ✓

> **Example — Potential breach:** "Dr Lopez (Chiropractor) is a specialist in paediatric chiropractic care."  
> **Correct:** "Dr Lopez (Chiropractor) has substantial experience working with musculoskeletal issues in children."

### 4.1.4.d — Qualifications

It is acceptable to advertise postgraduate qualifications, specific experience, or courses completed in an accurate and factual manner.

### 4.1.4.g — Use of the Title "Doctor"

"Doctor" is not a protected title, but practitioners must be careful because the public historically associates "Dr" with medical practitioners. If "Dr" is used by a non-medical practitioner, **the profession must be clearly stated in the advertising.**

> **Example — Potential breach:** "Dr Lee"  
> **Correct:** "Dr Lee (Osteopath)"
