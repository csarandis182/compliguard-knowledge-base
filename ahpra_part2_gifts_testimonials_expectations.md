## Section 4.2 — Gifts, Discounts or Inducements

**National Law section 133(1)(b):** A person must not advertise a regulated health service in a way that offers a gift, discount or other inducement to attract a person to use the service or the business, **unless the advertisement also states the terms and conditions of the offer.**

### Requirements

- Terms and conditions must be stated in **plain language**
- If it is not possible to display full terms and conditions in the advertisement, the offer must **direct the public to the location of the terms and conditions** (e.g. via a link or website reference)
- Terms and conditions must be **easily found and accessible** — the public should not be required to exhaustively search for or contact the advertiser for terms and conditions

### What May Breach the National Law

Advertising that:
- Contains price information that is **unclear, inexact or vague**
- Offers "make one consultation, get one free" but **raises the price** of the first consultation to largely cover the cost of the second
- **Excludes existing restrictions or limitations** such as age, expiry date, geographical restrictions, or who is eligible for the offer
- States an instalment amount **without stating the total cost**

> **Key note:** The public generally considers the word "free" to mean "absolutely" free. When the costs of a "free offer" are recouped through a price rise elsewhere or through other sources such as Medicare, the offer is not actually free.

## Section 4.3 — Testimonials

**National Law section 133(1)(c):** A person must not advertise a regulated health service in a way that **uses testimonials or purported testimonials** about the service or business.

This specifically prohibits: patient stories and experiences, success stories, and fake testimonials.

### Section 4.3.1 — What Is a Testimonial?

A testimonial is a **positive statement about a person or thing** used in advertising. In the context of the National Law, testimonials are **recommendations or positive statements about the clinical aspects of a regulated health service** used in advertising.

**A clinical aspect exists if one of the following is expressed:**
- **Symptom** — the specific symptom or the reason for seeking treatment
- **Diagnosis or treatment** — the specific diagnosis or treatment provided by the practitioner
- **Outcome** — the specific outcome or the skills or experience of the practitioner, either directly or via comparison

**Not a testimonial:** Comments about customer service or communication style that do **not** include a reference to clinical aspects.

### Section 4.3.2 — Are Patient Reviews Permitted?

Patient reviews may appear on platforms the practitioner does not control (Google, independent forums, third-party directories). The prohibition only applies when:
- An **advertiser makes use of testimonials to advertise** a regulated health service; and/or
- A person or business **advertises in a way that makes use of reviews/testimonials** to promote the service

**The prohibition does NOT affect:**
- Patients sharing their views online or posting reviews on review platforms
- How members of the public interact with review sites or discussion forums
- Individuals or businesses that do not advertise a regulated health service

### Section 4.3.3 — Who Is Responsible?

The **advertiser** (whoever has control over the advertising) is responsible for compliance.

| Situation | Responsible Party |
|---|---|
| Review appearing on clinic/practitioner's own website | Clinic owner or practitioner |
| Review on clinic/practitioner's business social media | Clinic owner or practitioner |
| Review on third-party site where practitioner has no control | Owner of the third-party platform |
| Review on third-party site where practitioner has other content | Practitioner is responsible for their own content, not the review |

> **Key compliance action:** If testimonials/reviews appear on your own website or social media, you must remove them or disable the review function. For social media platforms that don't allow removal, disabling the reviews/testimonials function is required.

## Section 4.4 — Advertising That Creates an Unreasonable Expectation of Beneficial Treatment

**National Law section 133(1)(d):** A person must not advertise a regulated health service in a way that **creates an unreasonable expectation of beneficial treatment.**

### Examples of Potential Breaches

Advertising may breach this requirement where it:
- Creates an unreasonable expectation of **outcomes or recovery time** (by exaggerating or providing incomplete/biased information)
- **Overstates the potential benefit** of a treatment
- Minimises the complexity or risk associated with a treatment by using words or phrases such as **"safe", "effective", "risk-free", "pain-free"** without acknowledging possible adverse reactions or mixed/inconclusive evidence
- Contains **false or unsubstantiated information** that is likely to make a person believe their health may suffer from not using the service
- Either expressly or by omission, indicates that the treatment is **infallible, unfailing, magical, miraculous, or a guaranteed/sure cure**
- States that the practitioner has an **exclusive or unique skill or remedy** that will benefit the patient
- Uses **photos or images of unrealistic outcomes**

### Section 4.4.1 — Images and Photographs

Care must be taken with graphic or visual representations to ensure they do not create unreasonable expectations.

Advertising may breach this section if:
- It is not clear **how the advertised treatment caused the benefit shown** in the image
- Images are **not genuine** and/or have been edited or enhanced

**"Before and after" images** are high-risk. They are less likely to be misleading if:
- Images are as similar as possible in content, camera angle, background, framing and exposure
- Posture, clothing and make-up is consistent
- Lighting and contrast is consistent
- There is an explanation if images have been altered in any way
- The referenced treatment or procedure is the **only visible change** to the person being photographed

## Section 4.5 — Encouraging Indiscriminate or Unnecessary Use

**National Law section 133(1)(e):** A person must not advertise a regulated health service in a way that **directly or indirectly encourages the indiscriminate or unnecessary use of regulated health services.**

### Examples of Potential Breaches

Advertising may be unlawful when it:
- Creates a **sense of urgency** linked to health suffering if a service is not used, without clinical indication. This includes phrases such as: **"don't delay", "act now before it's too late", "don't miss out", "time is running out", "for a limited time only"**
- **Encourages periodic or regular appointments** where there is no clinical indication to do so — including contracting for future services
- Uses **incentives such as prizes, discounts, bonuses or gifts** that would encourage people to use services regardless of clinical need or therapeutic benefit, particularly where the value of the prize greatly outweighs the cost of the treatment

> **Example — Potential breach:** "Each time you attend for cosmetic injections at our practice you go into the draw to win a luxury car. The more times you attend the more entries you get and the more chances you have to win!"
