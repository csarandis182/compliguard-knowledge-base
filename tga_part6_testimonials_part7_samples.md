## Part 6 — Testimonials and Endorsements (Section 24)

### 24(1) — General Rule

A testimonial or endorsement used in an advertisement about therapeutic goods must comply with this section and all other applicable provisions of this Code.

### 24(2) — Consistency Requirement

A testimonial or endorsement must not be inconsistent with:
- **(a)** the label for the goods; and
- **(b)** the directions for use or the instructions for use; and
- **(c)** an indication or intended purpose that is accepted in relation to the inclusion of the goods in the Register (for registered goods).

### 24(3) — Typicality Requirement

If a testimonial or endorsement refers, **expressly or by implication, to a health benefit**, then the health benefit must be **typical of the benefit expected** from the goods when used in accordance with the label, directions for use, and the accepted indication.

> **Compliance note:** Exceptional individual results ("I lost 15kg in 2 weeks!") are non-compliant if they are not typical of average consumer outcomes. This rule applies even to real testimonials.

### 24(4) — Prohibited Testimonial Sources

An advertisement must not contain a testimonial made by any of the following:

- **(a)** a person who is **engaged in the production, marketing or supply of the goods** (a "relevant person") — **this includes influencers, direct sellers, and any person who receives valuable consideration** for the testimonial; or
- **(b)** a member of a relevant person's **immediate family**, unless the advertisement discloses the family relationship; or
- **(c)** a person or organisation mentioned in (6)(a) to (e) [see endorsement prohibitions below]; or
- **(d)** a **corporation.**

### 24(5) — Verification Requirement

An advertisement must not contain a testimonial unless the advertiser has:
- **verified the content** of the testimonial; and
- **verified the identity** of the person making the testimonial.

### 24(6) — Prohibited Endorsement Sources

An endorsement must not be given, whether expressly or by implication, by any of the following:

- **(a)** a **government or government authority**, unless otherwise permitted by the Act or Regulations; or
- **(b)** a **hospital or healthcare facility**, other than a community pharmacy; or
- **(c)** **employees or contractors** of a body mentioned in (a) or (b); or
- **(d)** a **current or former health practitioner, health professional or medical researcher**; or
- **(e)** a person who **represents themselves as being qualified or trained to diagnose, treat or prevent disease**, ailment, defect or injury; or
- **(f)** an **organisation that represents the interests of healthcare consumers**, or represents health practitioners — unless the advertisement discloses: (i) the name of the organisation; and (ii) whether the organisation has received, or will receive, any valuable consideration for the endorsement.

> **Compliance note:** A doctor, nurse, physiotherapist, or any registered health professional cannot be used as an endorser in consumer-facing advertising for therapeutic goods. This is one of the most commonly violated provisions in digital health marketing.

## Part 7 — Samples and Incentives

### Section 25 — Samples

An advertisement must not contain or consist of a sample, or an offer of a sample, of therapeutic goods, unless:
- **(a)** the goods are listed in **Annexure 2**; and
- **(b)** any conditions for that item are met; and
- **(c)** the goods do not contain a substance in Schedule 2, 3, 4 or 8 to the current Poisons Standard; and
- **(d)** for registered goods — the goods are supplied in a pack accepted in relation to the inclusion of the goods.

> **Note:** A "buy one, get one free" arrangement is not considered a sample, provided the free goods are the same as the purchased goods.

### Section 26 — Incentives

An advertisement about therapeutic goods must **not offer any personal incentive or commission to a pharmacy assistant, or any retail salesperson who is not a health professional**, in exchange for recommending or supplying the goods.
