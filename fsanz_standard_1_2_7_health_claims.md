# FSANZ Food Standards Code — Standard 1.2.7: Nutrition, Health and Related Claims

**Source:** Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code — Standard 1.2.7  
**Administered by:** Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ)  
**Applies to:** Food products sold in Australia (including dietary supplements, functional foods, fortified foods)  
**chunk_id:** fsanz-standard-1-2-7-health-claims

> **Important note:** FSANZ Standard 1.2.7 does not apply to therapeutic goods regulated by the TGA. The Food-Medicine Interface (see separate chunk) determines whether a product is regulated as food (FSANZ) or as a therapeutic good (TGA). Many VDS (vitamins, dietary supplements) products can fall under either framework depending on how they are presented and marketed.

---

## Overview

Standard 1.2.7 regulates **nutrition content claims and health claims** made about food products in Australia and New Zealand. It establishes:

- What types of claims are **permitted** on food labels and in food advertising
- The **conditions** that must be met to make each claim type
- Which claims are **prohibited** regardless of evidence

---

## Types of Claims Under Standard 1.2.7

### 1. Nutrition Content Claims

A **nutrition content claim** is a claim about the presence or absence of a nutrient or substance in a food, or a property of a food related to its nutritional value.

**Examples:**
- "High in protein"
- "Low in fat"
- "Reduced sodium"
- "Good source of calcium"
- "Contains Omega-3"
- "No added sugar"
- "Gluten free"

**Requirements:**
- The food must meet the **relevant threshold** in the Standard (e.g., to claim "high in protein," the food must contain a minimum amount of protein per serving and per 100g/mL)
- Exact thresholds for each claim type are set out in Schedule 1 of Standard 1.2.7
- "Free from" claims (e.g., "fat free", "sugar free", "gluten free") require the substance to be below the prescribed threshold — typically very low or undetectable levels

---

### 2. General Level Health Claims

A **general level health claim** refers to a **nutrient or substance in a food** and a **health effect** of that nutrient or substance. It does not refer to a serious disease or biomarker of a serious disease.

**Examples:**
- "Calcium is good for strong bones and teeth"
- "Vitamin C contributes to the normal function of the immune system"
- "Iron supports normal energy metabolism"

**Requirements:**
- The claim must be based on a **pre-approved food-health relationship** (listed in the Standard)
- The food must meet the **Nutrient Profiling Scoring Criterion (NPSC)** — this is a scoring system ensuring only nutritionally appropriate foods can make health claims. Foods that are high in saturated fat, sodium or added sugars will fail the NPSC and **cannot make health claims** regardless of their other nutritional attributes
- The claim must be expressed accurately and not be misleading

---

### 3. High Level Health Claims (Biomarker Claims)

A **high level health claim** refers to a **serious disease or a biomarker of a serious disease**.

**Examples:**
- "Diets high in calcium may reduce the risk of osteoporosis in people 65 years and older" (diet-disease relationship)
- "Plant sterols can help reduce cholesterol, a risk factor for heart disease" (biomarker claim)

**Requirements:**
- Must be based on a **pre-approved food-health relationship** in the Standard
- Must include a **mandatory disclaimer** that a healthy diet and lifestyle are also important
- Food must meet the **NPSC**
- Must include the **full, pre-approved wording** of the claim — paraphrasing or shortening the approved claim is not permitted

---

### 4. Systematic Reviews

Standard 1.2.7 permits the TGA and FSANZ to update permitted claim lists based on systematic reviews of evidence. Businesses **cannot unilaterally introduce new health claims** based on their own evidence assessment — they must use pre-approved claim wording.

---

## Prohibited Claims Under Standard 1.2.7

The following are **prohibited** regardless of whether they are technically accurate:

- Claims that a food **treats, cures or prevents** a disease or condition (these are therapeutic claims and fall under TGA jurisdiction, not FSANZ)
- Claims that **create an unreasonable expectation of benefit** from consuming the food
- Health claims on foods that **fail the NPSC** (e.g., a high-sugar beverage cannot claim immune support benefits)
- Health claims on **infant formula** and related infant products (subject to special rules)
- Nutrition content claims that **do not meet the prescribed thresholds** in the Standard

---

## Interaction with TGA Advertising Code

**Critical boundary:** If a food product is presented in a way that suggests therapeutic use (treatment, cure, prevention of a disease), it may be classified as a **therapeutic good** under the TGA framework rather than — or in addition to — being regulated as food under FSANZ.

The key question is how the product is **presented and marketed**:
- "Supports healthy immune function" (food/general health claim — may be FSANZ)
- "Treats recurring colds and flu" (therapeutic claim — TGA jurisdiction, restricted representation)

Refer to the Food-Medicine Interface Guidance Tool (separate chunk) to determine which framework applies to a specific product.

---

## NPSC (Nutrient Profiling Scoring Criterion) Summary

The NPSC assigns scores to foods based on their nutrient composition. Foods with **high scores (indicating poor nutritional profile)** cannot make health claims.

Factors that increase (worsen) the score:
- Energy (kJ per 100g)
- Saturated fat (g per 100g)
- Total sugars (g per 100g)
- Sodium (mg per 100g)

Factors that decrease (improve) the score:
- Fruit, vegetable, nut and legume content (%)
- Dietary fibre (g per 100g)
- Protein (g per 100g)

**Threshold:** Foods scoring at or above the category threshold **cannot make health claims**, even if they contain a beneficial nutrient.

## Quick Reference: Claim Types

| Claim Type | Disease Reference? | NPSC Required? | Pre-approved Wording Required? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nutrition content claim | No | No | No (must meet threshold) |
| General level health claim | No (health effect only) | Yes | Yes (pre-approved relationship) |
| High level health claim | Yes (disease or biomarker) | Yes | Yes (exact pre-approved wording) |
| Therapeutic claim ("treats X") | Yes | N/A — **TGA jurisdiction** | N/A |
