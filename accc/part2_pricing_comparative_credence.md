# ACCC Advertising and Selling Guide 2021 — Part 2: Pricing, Comparative Advertising & Credence Claims

**Source:** ACCC Advertising and Selling Guide, July 2021  
**Legislative basis:** Australian Consumer Law (ACL), Competition and Consumer Act 2010  
**Key sections:** ACL ss. 29, 32, 35, 47, 48, 49  
**chunk_id:** accc-part2-pricing-comparative-credence

---

## Component Pricing (ACL s.48)

When presenting prices, you must state the **total price** as a single figure. This applies to advertising across **all mediums** including online.

If you promote a price that is only part of the total price, you must also include the total price at least **as prominently** as the part price.

The total single price must include:
- Any tax, duty, fee, levy or other additional charges (e.g. GST)
- Any optional fees or charges **pre-selected** for the consumer during the purchasing process (unless de-selected by the consumer)

> **Example:** A supplement costs $40. There is a mandatory $5 handling fee and 10% GST. The advertisement must display a prominent total of $49.50, not just "$40."

---

## Two-Price Comparison Advertising ("Was/Now" and Strike-Through Pricing)

Using "was $150/now $100" or strike-through pricing represents that consumers will **save the difference** between the two prices. This is misleading if:

- The product had **not been offered for sale at the "was" price** before the sale commenced
- The product was **rarely or never sold at the "was" price** (e.g., business has an established discounting practice)
- The "sale" price has been in place for so long that it **effectively becomes the new selling price**

**Key principle:** A "sale" or "discounted" price should only be available for a **limited period.** If a reasonable time has elapsed and the item is still "on sale," continuing to call it a "discount" may be misleading.

**Record keeping:** Keep records substantiating any 2-price comparison claim. ACCC regulators have the power to compel you to substantiate such claims.

## Comparative Advertising

Businesses may use comparative advertising to promote superiority over competitors in price, quality, range or volume. Before using comparative advertising, consider:

- Is the comparison **accurate**?
- Are the products or services being compared **reasonably similar**?
- Will the comparison be **valid for the life of the promotion**? (Competitors may change their products in response.)

Comparisons must not be misleading by omitting important information that would qualify the comparison.

## Bait Advertising (ACL s.35)

It is illegal to advertise goods or services at a discounted price when they are **not available in reasonable quantities and for a reasonable period** at that price. You must:

- State clearly if the good is in short supply or on sale for a limited time
- Promptly offer a "rain check," acceptable substitute product, or take other corrective action if you run out

---

## Gifts, Rebates and Prizes (ACL s.32)

It is unlawful to:
- Offer rebates, gifts, prizes or other free items **without intending to provide them**
- **Fail to provide them as promised**

A rebate or gift must be provided within the specified time or, if no time was specified, within a reasonable time.

## Health Claims and Nutritional Claims

Advertisements for health and medical products and services can have a powerful influence on consumers. All health claims must be **accurate and truthful** — you must have **reasonable grounds** before making any effectiveness claim.

**ACCC and FSANZ interaction:** The ACL is complemented by **Standard 1.2.7 of the Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code** which regulates what nutritional and health claims can be made about certain foods. Health claims on food products must comply with **both** ACL and FSANZ requirements.

### Claims About Future Outcomes

A business making claims about future matters (e.g., predicted health outcomes, expected results) must have **reasonable grounds** for doing so at the time of making the claim. If it does not, the business can be guilty of misleading or deceptive conduct.

## Premium Claims and Credence Claims

**Credence claims** are claims the consumer cannot independently verify themselves and must trust the seller. Examples include:

- "100% natural"
- "Pharmaceutical grade"
- "Clinically tested"
- "Doctor recommended"
- "Australian made / Australian grown"
- "Certified organic"
- "Free from [ingredient]"

**Requirements:**
- All credence claims must be **substantiated** before publication
- The word "free" (e.g., "gluten free", "preservative free") is **absolute** — if the product contains even trace amounts of the substance, the claim is misleading
- "Not tested on animals" / "cruelty free" claims must accurately reflect **all stages of production**, including by suppliers and in export markets

## Environmental and Organic Claims

### Environmental Claims

Environmental claims (e.g., "green", "environmentally safe", "biodegradable", "100% recyclable") must be:
- **Honest, accurate and substantiated**
- **Clearly explained** in simple language — broad terms like "environmentally friendly" may mislead because they have more than one meaning

### Organic Claims

An "organic" claim is any claim that describes a product or its ingredients as organic. Requirements:
- Must be **substantiated** regardless of whether a certification is held
- While organic certification is not legally required, you must be able to justify the claim
- Claims like "100% organic", "made using organic ingredients" or "certified organic" must accurately describe the product

## Country of Origin Claims

All businesses are **prohibited from making false or misleading representations about the origin of goods** (both food and non-food products).

### "Made in Australia" (Safe Harbour)

For the safe harbour defence, the goods must have been **substantially transformed** in Australia — meaning the end product is fundamentally different in identity, nature or essential character from all of its imported ingredients or components. Mere changes to form or appearance of imported goods do **not** satisfy the substantial transformation test.

### "Product of Australia" / "Produce of Australia" (Safe Harbour)

To use this claim, **each significant component or ingredient** must have originated in Australia, and **all or virtually all** of the production processes must have taken place in Australia.

## Bait Advertising, Referral Selling and Wrongful Payment

| Practice | Status |
|---|---|
| Selling below-advertised goods when not available in reasonable supply | Prohibited — bait advertising (s.35) |
| Offering benefits to consumers contingent on other consumers also purchasing | Prohibited — referral selling (s.49) |
| Accepting payment for goods/services without intending or being able to supply them | Prohibited — wrongfully accepting payment (s.36) |
