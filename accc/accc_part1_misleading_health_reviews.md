# ACCC Advertising and Selling Guide 2021 — Part 1: Misleading Conduct, Health Claims & Reviews

**Source:** ACCC Advertising and Selling Guide, July 2021  
**Legislative basis:** Australian Consumer Law (ACL), Competition and Consumer Act 2010  
**Key sections:** ACL ss. 18, 29, 32, 35  
**chunk_id:** accc-part1-misleading-health-reviews

---

## The Two Fundamental Rules of Advertising (ACL)

1. You must **not engage in conduct that is likely to mislead or deceive** (ACL s.18)
2. You must **not make false or misleading claims or statements** (ACL s.29)

These rules apply equally to all advertising channels including **online, social media, apps, websites, email, search engines and social media advertising**. Businesses operating online have the same obligations as bricks-and-mortar stores.

**Intent is irrelevant:** It is illegal to engage in misleading or deceptive conduct **even if you did not intend to mislead anyone** and even if no one has suffered loss or damage.

---

## Misleading or Deceptive Conduct (ACL s.18)

### The Overall Impression Test

When deciding if conduct is misleading or deceptive, the most important question is whether the **overall impression** created by your conduct is false or inaccurate. All relevant circumstances are taken into account:
- The entire advertisement (not just one clause)
- Product labels
- Statements made by sales representatives
- Fine print, contradictory statements, and images that obscure or alter written statements

### Misleading by Omission

A business must provide information to avoid misleading or deceptive conduct where its other conduct has created a misleading impression, or **where it is reasonable to expect that the information will be disclosed.** Silence can constitute misleading conduct.

### "Free" Claims

Businesses must be particularly careful with the word **"free"**. Consumers will usually think of "free" as absolutely free. Businesses may breach the ACL if they:
- Offer "10% free" but raise the base price to cover the extra volume
- Make a "buy one, get one free" offer but raise the price of the first item to largely cover the cost of the second

### Puffery (Not Misleading)

**Puffery** is wildly exaggerated, fanciful or vague claims that no one could possibly treat seriously. These are generally not considered misleading under the ACL.
> Example: "The best steaks on earth" or "the tastiest food in town" — considered puffery.

### Fine Print and Disclaimers

Fine print does **not** automatically protect a business from breaching the ACL. An advertiser must not make the real terms and conditions unclear by:
- Placing text in obscure locations
- Using text that is too small
- Flashing disclaimers on screen for only a moment
- Using voice-overs that are too quick or too quiet

The main selling point may create such a strong impression that **no disclaimer can dispel it.**

## False or Misleading Claims (ACL s.29)

Specific prohibitions include claims that:
- Goods are of a particular **standard, quality, value, grade, composition, style or model** when they are not
- Goods have had a particular **history or particular previous use** when they have not
- Services have particular **characteristics, uses or benefits** which they do not have

## Health Claims and Other Benefits

> **Critical section for VDS/health products marketing**

Advertisements for health and medical services, and the benefits they provide, can have a powerful influence on consumers. It is **essential that businesses provide accurate and truthful information** so consumers can make informed decisions.

### Requirements for Health Claims

- Every health or benefit claim must be **substantiated** — you must have reasonable grounds before making the claim
- Claims about effectiveness must have an **evidence base** — anecdotal evidence or internal testing without controls is insufficient
- Claims about future outcomes (e.g. "you will lose 5kg") must be based on **reasonable grounds** at the time of making the claim

### Nutritional and Health Claims About Food Products

**The ACL is complemented by Standard 1.2.7 of the Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code**, which regulates what nutritional and health claims can be made about certain foods. (See FSANZ chunk for detail.)

## Reviews and Testimonials

### The Genuine Review Rule

Any review or testimonial must reflect the **genuine views and opinions** of the person represented to have made it. Using false or misleading reviews or testimonials in any advertising medium risks contravening the ACL.

A **fake review** is one which does not reflect the genuinely held opinion of the author.

### Prohibited Review Practices

Businesses may be engaging in misleading or deceptive conduct if they:
- **Use fake reviews** — including as false advertising or to damage a competitor's reputation
- Use tactics to **influence a consumer to provide a positive review** or refrain from a negative review
- **Selectively remove or edit reviews**, particularly negative reviews, for commercial or promotional reasons
- **Omit negative reviews** — this can be just as misleading as posting fake reviews

### Social Media Responsibility

Businesses using social media are responsible for **content on their pages, irrespective of who posted it**. You can be held responsible for posts or public comments made by others on your social media pages which are false or likely to mislead.

You must **monitor your social media pages and remove any posts that are false, misleading or deceptive** as soon as you become aware of them. Display a clear and prominent moderation policy.

> **Key case:** A company was found to have accepted responsibility for fan posts and testimonials on its social media pages when it knew about them and chose not to remove them. (Federal Court — [2011] FCA 74)

### Online Review Platforms

Fake online reviews are in breach of the ACL regardless of whether they appear on your own website or a third-party review platform. Businesses must not write or commission reviews about their own business or a competitor's business that are misleading.

## Premium and Credence Claims

**Premium claims** are claims that give the impression a product has some kind of added benefit compared to similar products. Common types:

- Superior claims: "extra virgin olive oil", "pharmaceutical grade"
- Nutritional benefit claims: "no added colours or preservatives", "high in protein"
- Perceived quality by origin: "Swiss formulated", "Australian made"
- Negative-attribute claims: "free from [ingredient]" — the word "free" is absolute; if the product contains even trace amounts of the thing it claims to be free from, the claim is misleading

### Substantiation Requirement

Premium claims may influence consumers' purchasing decisions. As consumers are often unable to assess the accuracy of premium claims, you **must ensure that the claims you make can be substantiated** before publishing.

---

## Advertising Agents and Marketing Agencies

When a business uses an agent (marketing agency, social media manager, call centre), **the business will generally be responsible for the agent's conduct.** A business should supervise the activities of its agents and avoid inadvertently breaching the ACL through third-party content.
