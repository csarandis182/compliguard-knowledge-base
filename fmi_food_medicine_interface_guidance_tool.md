# Food-Medicine Interface (FMI) Guidance Tool

**Source:** Therapeutic Goods Administration — Food-Medicine Interface Guidance Tool Diagram  
**Published by:** TGA, Department of Health  
**Reference document:** informationfmigt140729.pdf  
**chunk_id:** fmi-guidance-tool

## Purpose

The Food-Medicine Interface Guidance Tool helps determine whether a product is regulated as a **"therapeutic good"** (TGA jurisdiction) or as **"food"** (FSANZ/state-territory jurisdiction). This distinction is critical because it determines:

- Which regulatory framework applies to the product
- What claims can be made about the product
- Which compliance rules govern advertising

---

## Why This Matters for VDS Marketing

Many vitamins, dietary supplements and complementary medicines sit at the intersection of the food and medicine regulatory frameworks. A product that is presented or marketed in certain ways may trigger TGA regulation even if it is sold as a food or supplement. The **way a product is advertised can determine which regulator has jurisdiction.**

---

## The Decision Flow (7 Questions)

Work through these questions in order. Stop at the first "Yes" that reaches a definitive outcome.

---

### Q1: Is the product for oral use for humans?

- **No** → The issue of the food interface does not arise. (The product may still be a therapeutic good by another pathway, but not via the food interface.)
- **Yes** → Proceed to Q2.

---

### Q2: Is there a (post-2010) section 7 declaration that the product is a therapeutic good?

Section 7 of the Therapeutic Goods Act 1989 allows the Secretary to declare that specified goods are therapeutic goods.

- **Yes** → **The product IS a therapeutic good**, not food. TGA rules apply.
- **No** → Proceed to Q3.

---

### Q3: Is the product covered by a section 7AA declaration?

Section 7AA allows the Secretary to declare that specified goods are NOT therapeutic goods.

- **Yes** → **The product is NOT a therapeutic good.** It may be "food" within state/territory food regulation legislation and/or regulated under other state/territory legislation. FSANZ rules apply.
- **No** → Proceed to Q4.

---

### Q4: Is the product "goods for which there is a standard" in the Food Standards Code?

Check whether FSANZ has a specific standard covering this product type (e.g., formulated supplementary sports foods, formulated meal replacements, infant formula, etc.).

- **Yes** → **The product is NOT a therapeutic good.** It is likely to be food within state/territory food regulation. FSANZ rules apply.
- **No** → Proceed to Q5.

---

### Q5: Does the product have a tradition of use as food for humans in the form in which it is presented?

This question considers whether the product, **in the form it is sold**, has a history of being consumed as food in Australia or New Zealand.

- **Yes** → **The product is NOT a therapeutic good.** It is likely food. FSANZ rules apply.
- **No** → Proceed to Q6.

---

### Q6: Is the product any of the following?

1. **Represented in any way to be for therapeutic use?**
2. **Likely to be taken to be for therapeutic use because of the way in which it is presented?**
3. **Likely to be taken to be for therapeutic use for any other reason?**

> **This is the critical marketing question.** If the **advertising or packaging** of the product makes therapeutic claims (treatment, cure, prevention of disease), the product becomes a therapeutic good regardless of its physical composition.

- **Yes** → **The product IS a therapeutic good**, not food. TGA rules apply, including the TGA Advertising Code.
- **No** → Proceed to Q7.

---

### Q7: Is the product in a class of goods whose sole or principal use is, or ordinarily is, a therapeutic use?

- **Yes** → **The product IS a therapeutic good**, not food. TGA rules apply.
- **No** → If the product is not a biological or medical device, **it is NOT a therapeutic good.** It may be food under state/territory food regulation legislation.

---

## Practical Implications for VDS Advertising

### Q6 is the highest-risk question for marketing teams

A product that passes Q1-Q5 as "food" can **become a therapeutic good through advertising alone** if the marketing:

- Claims the product **treats, prevents or cures** a specific health condition
- Presents the product using **therapeutic language** (e.g., "clinically proven to reduce inflammation")
- Creates an impression through packaging, imagery or copy that the product has **medical or therapeutic purposes**

### Common Scenarios

| Product | Marketed As | Likely Classification |
|---|---|---|
| Fish oil capsules | "Heart health support — for cardiovascular wellbeing" | Food/supplement (general health claim) |
| Fish oil capsules | "Reduces risk of cardiovascular disease in adults over 40" | Therapeutic good — restricted representation, TGA approval required |
| Turmeric supplement | "Traditionally used for anti-inflammatory support" | Therapeutic good — complementary medicine, traditional use claim |
| Turmeric powder | "Add to food for flavour and colour" | Food — FSANZ jurisdiction |
| Protein powder | "Supports muscle recovery after exercise" | Food — FSANZ (sports nutrition standard) |
| Protein powder | "Treats muscle wasting conditions" | Therapeutic good — TGA restricted representation required |

---

## Summary Decision Table

| Outcome | Framework | Advertising Rules |
|---|---|---|
| Product IS a therapeutic good | TGA | TGA Advertising Code 2021 applies in full |
| Product is NOT a therapeutic good (food) | FSANZ + state/territory | FSANZ Standard 1.2.7, ACCC ACL |
| Product advertised with therapeutic claims | TGA triggered via Q6 | TGA Advertising Code applies regardless of product composition |

---

## Key Principle

> **The way you market a product can change its regulatory classification.** A supplement that would otherwise be regulated as food becomes subject to TGA regulation if its advertising makes therapeutic claims — even if the product's formulation has not changed.

This is the core reason why marketing copy for VDS products must be reviewed against **both** FSANZ and TGA frameworks before publication.
